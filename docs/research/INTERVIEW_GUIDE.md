# Interview Guide: Fencing Solo-Practice Feedback

Status: Working guide
Last updated: 2026-04-21
Purpose: Collect stronger evidence for the AI-assisted fencing coaching problem framing.

## Goal

Use these interviews to strengthen the weakest part of the current research positioning: direct evidence that solo-practice fencers lack timely, actionable feedback and rely on workarounds that are delayed, inconsistent, or low quality.

Target evidence to collect:

- what learners actually do today when they are unsure whether they practiced correctly
- what kinds of mistakes they fail to notice on their own
- what coaches believe students most often miss when practicing without supervision
- whether a lightweight AI coaching tool would fit naturally into practice rather than add friction

## Participants

Minimum first round:

- Student 1: beginner or intermediate fencer who practices independently
- Student 2: another learner with a different training habit or weapon if possible
- Coach 1: coach or instructor who supervises individual or group practice

## Interview Principles

- Ask about actual recent practice, not hypothetical preference.
- Push for one concrete episode at a time.
- When someone says "sometimes" or "usually," ask for the most recent example.
- Do not sell the AI system too early. First understand the current workflow and pain.
- Capture exact phrases when they describe confusion, frustration, or wasted effort.

## Student Interview Guide

### Opening

1. Can you briefly describe how you usually practice fencing when a coach is not closely watching you?
2. In the past two weeks, what kinds of solo or lightly supervised practice have you done?

### Current Practice Workflow

3. Walk me through one recent practice session from start to finish.
4. During that session, when were you most unsure whether you were doing something correctly?
5. What did you do in that moment?

### Current Workarounds

6. When you are unsure about your movement, what do you usually rely on:
   - mirror
   - teammate
   - memory
   - waiting for a coach
   - recording video
   - something else
7. Which of those do you use most often?
8. Tell me about the most recent time that workaround did not help enough.

### Cost of Not Knowing

9. What usually happens if you keep practicing while unsure?
10. Have you ever realized later that you repeated a mistake many times? What was the mistake?
11. What is the real cost for you:
   - wasted time
   - building a bad habit
   - reduced confidence
   - confusion about what to fix
   - something else

### Feedback Timing and Format

12. If you could get feedback during solo practice, what would need to happen for it to actually help you in the next repetition?
13. What kind of feedback is easiest to use while practicing:
   - short voice feedback
   - visual cue
   - clip replay afterward
   - score or rating
   - something else
14. What kind of feedback would be annoying or make you stop using the system?

### Adoption and Fit

15. What would make you willing to use a phone-based feedback system every practice?
16. What would make you stop using it after trying it once or twice?
17. Compared with a mirror or waiting for a coach, what would this system have to do better to be worth using?

### Closing

18. If a system like this existed, what is the first thing you would want it to help you notice?
19. Is there a type of fencing mistake that learners often do not catch by themselves?

## Coach Interview Guide

### Opening

1. In your coaching experience, what kinds of mistakes do students most often fail to notice during independent practice?
2. Which students struggle most without immediate feedback: beginners, intermediates, or advanced learners?

### Current Coaching Constraints

3. In group training, when you are busy with one student, what usually happens to the others?
4. What kinds of corrections do students miss when you cannot watch them continuously?
5. What do students currently do to compensate when you are not available?

### Quality of Existing Workarounds

6. How effective are mirrors, teammate feedback, and delayed questions in your view?
7. Which of those works best, and where does it fail?
8. What kinds of errors are especially hard for students to self-diagnose?

### Useful Feedback Characteristics

9. If a lightweight AI system were used during solo practice, what kinds of feedback would actually be useful?
10. What would the system need to avoid doing to remain credible?
11. Would you trust action labels, short verbal corrections, highlighted video moments, or some combination?

### Positioning and Adoption

12. Do you see this kind of tool as:
   - coach replacement
   - coach assistant
   - solo-practice aid
   - video review aid
13. Which of those is the most realistic near-term role, and why?
14. What would make you recommend such a system to your students?
15. What would make you avoid recommending it?

### Closing

16. If you could give students one extra piece of feedback every time they practiced alone, what would it be?
17. What is the most important thing an AI fencing feedback system should do before you would take it seriously?

## What to Record

For each interview, record:

- participant role
- experience level
- practice context
- exact workaround examples
- exact mistakes they miss
- exact wording they use for pain or frustration
- adoption conditions
- trust concerns

Useful quote targets:

- "I thought I was doing it right until..."
- "When coach is busy, I just..."
- "The problem with using a mirror is..."
- "I would only use this if..."

## Evidence Checklist

After the first round, check whether you have:

- 2 concrete student examples of missed mistakes during solo practice
- 2 concrete workaround failures
- 1 coach account of what learners consistently miss
- 1 clear statement about the most realistic role of the system
- 1 adoption condition that is about workflow, not just model accuracy
