# Evidence Workflow: From Interview to Research Brief

Status: Working workflow
Last updated: 2026-04-21
Purpose: Turn the current research positioning into stronger evidence without getting stuck in open-ended note taking.

## 1. Objective

The current project already has:

- a plausible problem statement
- a related-work landscape
- a competitive positioning chart
- a draft research brief

The weakest link is still evidence quality. This workflow exists to raise the evidence tier by collecting a small but high-value first round of direct observations and interviews.

## 2. Minimum First Round

Complete this set before rewriting the research claim again:

- Student interview 1
- Student interview 2
- Coach interview 1
- 3 concrete workaround moments from practice

Do not try to scale before this round is done.

## 3. What Counts as Good Evidence

Strong first-round evidence looks like:

- a specific practice episode
- a specific missed mistake
- a specific workaround
- a specific cost of not knowing
- a specific adoption condition

Bad evidence looks like:

- "people probably want feedback"
- "students would like AI coaching"
- "coaches might use this"

## 4. Execution Workflow

### Step 1: Schedule

- find 2 students who actually practice independently
- find 1 coach who supervises group or individual training
- schedule 15-30 minute conversations

### Step 2: Run the Interview

Use:

- [INTERVIEW_GUIDE.md](INTERVIEW_GUIDE.md) for structure
- [INTERVIEW_SCRIPT_ZH.md](INTERVIEW_SCRIPT_ZH.md) for a natural spoken script

Rule:

- ask for recent real examples first
- only ask about hypothetical AI features near the end

### Step 3: Capture Raw Notes Immediately

Right after each interview, record:

- one concrete practice story
- one workaround example
- one workaround failure
- one quote about frustration or uncertainty
- one quote about what would make the tool worth using

Do this immediately. Do not trust memory later.

### Step 4: Extract Insights

After all 3 interviews, summarize:

- what students consistently miss
- which workaround is most common
- what cost appears most often
- what adoption barrier appears most often
- what role the system should realistically play

Use:

- [INTERVIEW_SYNTHESIS_TEMPLATE.md](INTERVIEW_SYNTHESIS_TEMPLATE.md)

### Step 5: Rewrite the Brief

Update:

- [RESEARCH_BRIEF.md](RESEARCH_BRIEF.md)
- [RELATED_WORK.md](RELATED_WORK.md)

Specifically:

- replace generic claims with participant quotes
- tighten primary-user language
- sharpen the strongest scenario
- remove any claim not supported by data

## 5. What You Are Trying to Learn

At the end of the first round, you should be able to answer:

1. What is the most common real-world failure mode during solo practice?
2. Which current workaround do students rely on most?
3. Why is that workaround insufficient?
4. What kind of feedback would students actually use during practice?
5. What makes coaches trust or distrust such a system?
6. Is this really a solo-practice aid, a coach assistant, or a review tool?

## 6. Decision Rule After the First Round

If the interviews clearly confirm:

- repeated unnoticed mistakes
- poor timing/quality of current feedback
- willingness to use a lightweight system if friction is low

Then keep the current positioning and move forward.

If the interviews instead show:

- students mostly rely on coaches and are fine waiting
- mirrors and teammates already work well enough
- the main value is for coaches rather than solo learners

Then reframe the project. Do not force the original claim if the evidence points elsewhere.

## 7. Recommended Output Template

After the first round, write a one-page synthesis using this structure:

- **Who we talked to**
- **What they do today**
- **Most common workaround**
- **Most common failure**
- **Most important quote**
- **What this means for our problem framing**
- **What this changes in the brief**

Use:

- [INTERVIEW_SYNTHESIS_TEMPLATE.md](INTERVIEW_SYNTHESIS_TEMPLATE.md)

## 8. Common Failure Patterns to Avoid

- collecting long interviews but no concrete episodes
- asking too much about the imagined system and not enough about current behavior
- overgeneralizing from one vivid quote
- treating interest as adoption
- rewriting the whole research framing before the first evidence round is complete
