## Proposal / Paper Opening Draft

Fencing students often practice without continuous access to expert feedback. In solo practice, and even during lightly supervised group training, learners may repeat incorrect footwork or distance-management behaviors without realizing the mistake in the moment. Existing workarounds such as mirrors, teammate comments, or waiting to ask a coach later do provide some support, but they are often delayed, inconsistent, or weakly aligned with the precise moment when the error occurs. As a result, students risk reinforcing poor habits while spending practice time on repetitions that are less effective than they appear.

Prior fencing-specific computing work shows that fencing actions can be recognized, segmented, and tactically analyzed, but it does not yet establish a learner-facing AI coaching experience for everyday practice. In parallel, HCI and sports-coaching systems in other domains show that pose estimation, visual feedback, and knowledge-guided AI can support movement learning, yet these systems are not built around fencing-specific technique or coaching vocabulary. This leaves an opening for a system that combines fencing specificity, commodity-camera accessibility, and actionable feedback for skill refinement during independent practice.

This project investigates an AI-assisted fencing coaching system for solo practice. Rather than treating action recognition as the end goal, the system uses ordinary video, pose-based movement analysis, and interpretable feedback to help learners notice and correct mistakes they would otherwise miss. The central HCI question is not whether the model can label fencing movement, but whether students and coaches find the resulting feedback timely, understandable, and useful enough to change practice behavior.

## Formal Proposal Introduction Draft

Fencing instruction depends on timely expert feedback. However, such feedback is not continuously available when students practice on their own or when coaches must divide attention across multiple learners. In these moments, students may repeat incorrect footwork, spacing, or timing behaviors without recognizing the mistake at the point of performance. The consequence is not simply temporary confusion: practice time can be consumed by repetitions that reinforce weak technique rather than correct it.

Students already use several workarounds to cope with this gap, including mirrors, teammate comments, self-video, and delayed questions for coaches. These strategies are not useless, but they are uneven in quality and often poorly aligned with the exact moment in which the movement error occurs. Mirrors privilege visible form over interactional timing and distance. Teammate feedback varies with peer expertise. Delayed questions rely on the learner accurately remembering what felt wrong after the moment has passed. Taken together, these workarounds suggest that the problem is not the total absence of feedback, but the limited immediacy, specificity, and reliability of feedback during independent practice.

Prior research addresses parts of this problem without yet resolving the full learning experience. Fencing-specific systems show that actions can be recognized, segmented, and tactically visualized, but these systems primarily support recognition or expert analysis rather than learner-facing coaching in everyday practice. Meanwhile, HCI and sports-coaching systems in other domains demonstrate that pose estimation, feedback design, and AI-assisted guidance can support movement learning, yet these systems are not grounded in fencing-specific technique, vocabulary, or training structure. Commercial video-review tools further show the value of lightweight capture and asynchronous coaching workflows, but they similarly stop short of providing fencing-specific AI feedback for solo practice.

This proposal investigates an AI-assisted fencing coaching system for solo practice using commodity-camera video, pose-based movement analysis, and interpretable feedback. The goal is not to position action recognition as the contribution by itself, nor to claim that AI replaces coaches. Instead, the project asks whether AI-generated feedback can help students notice and correct mistakes they would otherwise miss during independent training. Framed as an HCI contribution, the research focuses on improving the feedback experience of solo fencing practice: making it more timely, understandable, and actionable than the workarounds students rely on today.

## Problem Statement

In fencing training, especially during solo practice, students can repeatedly perform incorrect footwork or distance-management behaviors without noticing the mistake at the moment it happens. When coaches are occupied or absent, current workarounds such as using a mirror, sparring with teammates, or saving questions for later provide feedback that is delayed, inconsistent, or detached from the exact movement moment. As a result, errors can become habits and practice time can be wasted without the student realizing it.

## Who & Why Now

- **Target users:** beginner and intermediate fencing students doing solo or lightly supervised practice; secondary users are coaches who want students to get more structured feedback between direct coaching moments.
- **Current workaround:** mirror-based self-checking, asking teammates, and postponing questions until a coach is available.
- **What changed:** commodity cameras, practical pose estimation, and modern feedback-generation pipelines now make it plausible to deliver fencing-specific movement feedback from ordinary video instead of requiring specialized lab setups.

## Research Question(s)

1. How can an AI-assisted system provide fencing-specific feedback during solo practice in a way that students find timely, understandable, and actionable?
2. Can commodity-camera video, pose estimation, and action-recognition pipelines support feedback that helps students notice and correct errors they would otherwise miss?
3. How do students and coaches respond to AI-generated fencing feedback compared with existing workarounds such as mirrors, teammates, and delayed coach questions?

## Proposed Approach

Build an AI-assisted fencing coaching system that analyzes ordinary video of practice, extracts pose and movement patterns, identifies fencing-relevant actions or errors, and delivers interpretable feedback aligned with the moment of performance. The system should be designed first for solo practice rather than for replacing live coaching. Its core value is not just recognition accuracy, but whether the learner can use the feedback to adjust immediately and avoid reinforcing mistakes.

## Killer Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Solo Footwork Correction

A beginner or intermediate fencer sets a phone near the strip during solo footwork practice. After a short sequence, the system highlights a repeated issue such as stepping in before recovering distance and gives a short, fencing-specific correction. Instead of continuing to repeat the same mistake for several minutes, the student notices the pattern immediately and adjusts in the next repetition.

### Scenario 2: Between-Coach-Moments Practice Support

During a training session, the coach is occupied with other students. A learner performs repeated drills and receives lightweight, interpretable feedback from the system without waiting for the coach to return. When the coach does check in, the student can review the flagged moments and ask a more precise question rather than vaguely reporting confusion.
